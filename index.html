Differential Visibility and Narrative Framing of LGBT+ Identities in U.S. Online News Media: A Corpus Analysis of 954,433 Articles, 2016–2025

Yifan Chen  ·  Interactive Telecommunications Program, Tisch School of the Arts, New York University
Background

News media play a formative role in constructing public understanding of marginalized communities. Within the LGBT+ population, distinct identity subgroups — gay, lesbian, bisexual, and transgender — occupy markedly different positions in both demographic reality and mediated representation. Despite bisexual individuals constituting the largest single subgroup within the LGBT+ adult population in the United States (Gallup, 2025), systematic investigation into the asymmetric distribution of news coverage across these four identities over a sustained period remains sparse in the U.S. context.

Objectives

This study pursues three analytical objectives: (1) to quantify and compare the volume of news media coverage across four LGBT+ identity markers over a ten-year period (2016–2025); (2) to characterize the lexical and thematic environments in which each identity is discussed, drawing on high-frequency collocate analysis; and (3) to examine the relationship between political event cycles — specifically presidential administration transitions — and fluctuations in identity-specific coverage volume.

Methods

A corpus of 954,433 English-language online news articles was retrieved from Media Cloud, a non-commercial academic media monitoring platform, spanning January 2016 through December 2025. Articles were identified through Boolean keyword search isolating each of four target identity terms: gay, lesbian, bisexual/bisexuality, and transgender/transsexual. Weekly mention counts were aggregated from daily article totals to smooth short-term noise. For each identity, the top 100 most frequent collocating terms were extracted from a stratified sample of 5,000 articles per identity and analyzed using document frequency ratios. Population benchmarks were drawn from the Gallup 2025 national survey of LGBT+ adult identity.

Results

Coverage volume was markedly asymmetrical. Gay-coded articles (n = 489,001; 51.2% of corpus) outnumbered bisexual articles (n = 56,978; 6.0%) by a factor of 8.6, despite bisexual individuals constituting 56% of the LGBT+ adult population compared to gay individuals' 17%. Transgender coverage (n = 310,418; 32.5%) exhibited strong temporal correspondence with anti-trans legislative events, with peak coverage clustering around executive orders (January 2017; January 2025) and Supreme Court proceedings. Collocate analysis revealed distinct discursive frames for each identity: gay coverage centered on rights, pride, and political figures; lesbian coverage on legal proceedings and cultural representation; bisexual coverage on population health contexts (notably blood donation policy and HIV) and celebrity disclosure; transgender coverage on adversarial political terms including ban, military, athletes, and court, with high co-occurrence of the term trump (doc_ratio = 0.174).

Conclusions

These findings provide empirical evidence of structural bisexual erasure and crisis-contingent transgender visibility in U.S. online news media. The lexical environments surrounding each identity suggest that editorial framing reflects and reinforces distinct social positions: gay identity as normative LGBT+ default; lesbian identity as culturally legible but politically marginal; bisexual identity as demographically invisible; and transgender identity as predominantly defined through its relation to political opposition. Sustained investment in identity-disaggregated media monitoring is warranted to track whether these patterns persist as the political landscape continues to evolve.

Gay coverage maintains the highest baseline across the full decade. The two dominant peaks — Obergefell v. Hodges (June 2015) and the Pulse nightclub shooting (June 2016) — are large enough to compress the other three rows into near-invisibility at the shared scale.

Trans

Outside major headlines, trans coverage is sparse. But any legislative action triggers outsized spikes: North Carolina's HB2 (2016), Trump's rescission of school guidance (2017), the military ban (2017), and the accelerating wave of state-level care and sports bans from 2022 onward. Media attention to trans identity is contingent almost entirely on policy conflict.

Lesbian

Lesbian coverage is consistently low, rising only in cultural contexts — Pride Month, film releases, representation stories. Lesbian identity almost never drives coverage in political or policy debates. The absence from legislative cycles, which so consistently produce trans spikes, is itself a pattern worth noting.

Bisexual

Bisexual coverage reads as a near-flat line throughout the dataset. Small spikes correspond to narrow contexts: the 2014 FDA blood donation debate, Ryan Russell's NFL coming-out (2019), monkeypox vaccine equity (2022), the final FDA blood ban repeal (January 2023). None produced coverage visible alongside the Gay row at the shared scale.

05 — Narrative framing

What story is being told about each identity?

This thematic labelling follows the methodology developed by Ng et al. (PLOS ONE, 2024) for corpus-based media analysis. The finding in their Singapore study holds here too: even within an already marginalized demographic, certain subgroups occupy the discursive margins. Each identity ends up associated with a distinct narrative frame, not because that frame reflects the community's self-defined priorities, but because of what kinds of stories editors chose to tell, week after week, for a decade.

07 — Bisexual: a closer look

56,978 articles. A decade of decline.

The total bisexual count — 56,978 articles across ten years — sounds like a number. But it obscures a direction. Coverage has not stayed flat. It has been falling, year after year, since 2016. The New York Times published 706 bisexual-mentioning articles in 2016. By 2025, that number was 34. That is a 95% decline in a decade, at the country's most influential newspaper.

This is not a story about bisexual people becoming less newsworthy. It is a story about bisexual people never having been treated as newsworthy in the first place, and the brief window of coverage that existed closing as the media moved on.

Top 20 outlets by bisexual article volume
These are outlets that mentioned "bisexual" or "bisexuality" in at least one article. Volume does not indicate depth of coverage — many articles mention bisexual identity only in passing.

The outlet list reads like a who's who of mainstream American media. The New York Times, Reuters, The Guardian, the Los Angeles Times, the Washington Post. These are not fringe publications. They are the outlets that set the national news agenda. The fact that bisexual coverage is concentrated here, and yet so low in volume, is telling: this is not a question of obscure media missing the story. It is the mainstream finding it, and choosing not to file it.

The steepest decline came between 2016 and 2018, when overall volume dropped by nearly half. That period coincides with the peak of "LGBT" being subsumed into a single acronym as story — coverage of the community increased overall, but became less identity-specific. Bisexual people were folded into LGBT+ without being named. By 2025, coverage had fallen to its lowest point in the dataset. The community grew. The articles did not.

News coverage is not the only medium where bisexual representation has been contracting. GLAAD's annual Where We Are on TV report found that bisexual-plus characters in primetime television declined for three consecutive years, reaching 20% of all LGBT+ characters in the 2024–25 season — far below bisexual people's 56% share of the LGBT+ adult population. GLAAD's Studio Responsibility Index for 2025 found bisexual-plus characters in film had fallen to just 10% of all LGBT+ film characters. Transgender characters fared even worse: appearing in only two major studio films, both flagged by GLAAD for harmful stereotyping.

The pattern holds across media formats. News coverage falls. Television representation falls. Film representation falls. The community grows. The stories do not follow.

Data sources and methodology. The 954,433 figure is the sum of four separate Boolean searches on Media Cloud (U.S. national collection): gay (489,001) + transgender OR transsexual (310,418) + lesbian (98,036) + bisexual OR bisexuality (56,978). Each search ran for January 2016 through December 2025. These are article counts, not mention counts; a single article containing both "gay" and "lesbian" would be counted in both totals. Collocate frequencies were extracted from stratified samples of 5,000 articles per identity. Population benchmarks are from the Gallup 2025 national survey of LGBT+ adult identity, with the four identity categories normalized to sum to 100%. Thematic labelling follows the methodology of Ng, Yang et al. (2024), PLOS ONE e0300385.
